## Current customer, buyer, competitor, and market evidence

### Faculty need is real, but the value target is workload-safe redesign rather than generation alone

- College Board's February 2026 report on more than 3,000 U.S. faculty found 77% had used AI professionally, while 92% were concerned about AI-enabled plagiarism or dishonesty and more than 84% associated AI use with reduced critical thinking, originality, and deep engagement. Assignment redesign remains urgent, but human academic judgment is a core requirement: [College Board](https://newsroom.collegeboard.org/new-college-board-research-faculty-express-near-universal-concern-student-ai-use-undermines).
- In a current professor discussion, the requested job is explicitly “AI-resistant teaching that doesn't increase your workload.” Suggested redesigns trade homework, scaffolding, meetings, and in-class work against grading and teaching load. A useful package must preserve learning goals without merely moving effort elsewhere: [r/Professors](https://www.reddit.com/r/Professors/comments/1t3m42u/ai_resistant_teaching_that_doesnt_increase_your/).
- EDUCAUSE's 2026 higher-education work survey (1,960 respondents) found 70% selected automating repetitive processes and 65% selected offloading administrative burdens as promising opportunities. Only 13% said their institution measures ROI for work-related AI. Workflow Lab's measured run and comparison thesis is directionally strong, but it still needs product-specific evidence: [EDUCAUSE](https://www.educause.edu/research/2026/the-impact-of-ai-on-work-in-higher-education).

### Privacy and institution approval are purchase constraints, not optional trust copy

- The same EDUCAUSE survey found 52% selected use of data without consent and 51% selected insufficient data protection among urgent risks; only 54% were aware of work-related AI policies even though 94% had recently used AI. Product guidance must be institution-specific and must not imply that general policy text authorizes a workflow: [EDUCAUSE](https://www.educause.edu/research/2026/the-impact-of-ai-on-work-in-higher-education).
- Stanford began a campus pilot of ChatGPT Edu, Gemini Enterprise, and Claude for Education in June 2026 after research groups and individuals had been purchasing tools themselves. Stanford says the institution can provide better protection and pricing, requires users to verify outputs, and applies additional checkpoints to connectors and repeatable workflows: [Stanford University IT](https://uit.stanford.edu/news/new-ai-tools-stanford-arrive-june-30).
- Houston requires approved tools for identifiable student and institutional data, while Notre Dame exposes data classifications and prohibits restricted data in AI. These sources support a hard pilot data boundary and weaken any assumption that a consumer subscription can accept student work by default: [University of Houston](https://www.uh.edu/infotech/aisolutions/ai-tools-hub/), [Notre Dame](https://ai.nd.edu/ai-in-action/approved-ai-tools/).

### Competitors validate spending but make free trials, alternate payers, and course boundaries the baseline

- TeachingsByDesign lists a 14-day no-card trial and a $59/month individual faculty tier for course-coherence diagnostics and exports: [TeachingsByDesign](https://www.teachingsbydesign.com/).
- Course Companion starts at $29/month for individual instructors, includes course-bounded processing and basic analytics, and offers a 14-day no-card trial. It also sells department access from $2,000/year: [Course Companion](https://www.coursecompanion.io/).
- Kova is free for educators and charges students $11 per class per semester or $29/year; its semester pilot is free. Lectio is free during beta and plans course-semester or shared instructor/student pricing. These models weaken confidence that individual professors will default to a personal monthly subscription: [Kova](https://www.kovaedu.com/), [Lectio](https://lectio.study/pricing).
- EDUCAUSE charged $195 for members and $260 for nonmembers for a June 2026 instructional-design course that promises immediately applicable artifacts and adaptable workflows. This is evidence of one-time professional-development spend, not proof of personal subscription retention: [EDUCAUSE course](https://events.educause.edu/ai-for-instructional-design-collaborating-with-intelligent-systems/2026/online-5).

## Change threshold and decision

- **Verified problem:** the protocol exists, but its Markdown table could produce inconsistent or incomplete notes; there was no machine-checkable way to distinguish a completed pilot from missing fields, enforce the privacy boundary, exclude synthetic fixtures, or apply the five-pilot gates.
- **Why highest leverage:** direct pilot evidence is the critical path. Improving evidence integrity advances the decision without adding speculative product scope.
- **Expected outcome and metric:** 100% of completed pilots should produce schema-valid records with both sessions, data-boundary confirmation, comparable-task basis, correction result, usefulness, payer preference, unaided price thresholds, and commitment evidence. After five complete real records, the evaluator must return one precommitted cohort decision.
- **Why preferable to no change:** no product change is correct, but leaving evidence collection as a prose table risks missing fields and favorable interpretation. The new artifact makes the next customer interaction decision-ready.
- **Strongest alternative:** add accounts, analytics, AI revision, billing, or a new workflow. Those actions require approval or introduce material risk, and none resolves willingness-to-pay before a real pilot. A recruiting/outreach campaign would be more direct but is explicitly prohibited for autonomous execution.
- **Validation required:** schema checks, evaluator regression tests, synthetic exclusion, incomplete-record handling, negative-evidence retention, safety-stop behavior, and the complete product quality gate.

## Owner direction follow-up — simplicity and the first revenue bar

- The owner made ease a durable product constraint: extra knobs, manual workflow mapping, prompt writing, tool/model choices, and setup work will scare users away. The default experience must infer or prefill as much as safely possible and reveal detailed controls only when the preset fails.
- The first commercial target is at least $1,000 in monthly recurring revenue. This is a milestone for proving a small sustainable customer base, not permission to change pricing without approval.
- Subscriber equivalents are 53 at $19/month, 35 at $29/month, 26 at $39/month, 21 at $49/month, or 17 at $59/month. These are gross-revenue scenarios before payment fees, model usage, support, and hosting.
- The target is plausible in customer-count terms but unproven in demand terms. Workflow Lab currently has zero real professor pilots, zero payment commitments, and no retention data. Free institutional AI, free educator plans, and semester-funded alternatives make retention—not initial curiosity—the harder problem.
- Recommended path: keep the public journey preset-first; target a useful first result in under five minutes without operator help; validate one weekly or biweekly professor job; obtain at least two payment-oriented commitments from the first five complete pilots; then seek the first 10 paid subscribers before optimizing toward the price/customer combination required for $1,000 MRR.
