# Professor assignment-redesign pilot kit

Version: 1.0

Prepared: 2026-07-18

Status: Ready for one uncoached, low-risk professor pilot

## Decision this pilot must support

Determine whether Workflow Lab can help an individual university professor redesign one real assignment for current AI conditions, run the resulting workflow without operator help, convert a genuine miss into an acceptable human-approved correction, and show enough recurring value to justify discussing a subscription.

This is a validation protocol, not a claim that the product saves time or improves learning. Do not change product pricing or positioning from one pilot.

## Why this workflow

- Faculty are being asked to translate broad institutional AI guidance into course- and assignment-specific decisions while preserving learning objectives and academic judgment.
- Current professor discussions describe added workload from drafts, oral defenses, process evidence, custom rubrics, disclosure rules, and policy changes.
- University guidance emphasizes objective-first assignment redesign, approved tools, privacy, transparent AI-use boundaries, and human verification rather than AI detection.
- Individual alternatives now include focused workbooks and courses as well as professor-specific software. A current direct competitor, TeachingsByDesign, lists a 14-day trial and a $59/month faculty tier. That is a price anchor, not proof that Workflow Lab buyers will pay.

## Participant fit

Recruit one professor who:

- owns or can change one real assignment used in the next 30 days;
- has a current institutional, department, or course AI policy to consult;
- can describe the present redesign/review process and its approximate time;
- is willing to try the package twice and give candid feedback;
- can test without sharing student work, identifiable student data, confidential research, or restricted materials.

Exclude a participant when the test would require real grading, an academic-integrity allegation, student surveillance, an AI detector, protected data, or a decision the professor is not authorized to make.

## Hypotheses and disconfirming evidence

| Hypothesis | Evidence that strengthens it | Evidence that weakens it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The urgent job is translating policy and pedagogical intent into an executable assignment-redesign workflow. | The professor names this as a current problem and can point to repeated steps, rework, or uncertainty. | The problem is rare, already solved by the institution, or less important than another workflow. |
| The package is useful beyond a static template. | The professor can execute it uncoached, finds a real miss, and accepts a targeted correction that improves the second run. | The initial artifact is sufficient, the correction loop adds no value, or the professor needs a human consultant to operate it. |
| A subscription could earn renewal. | The professor expects repeated use across assignments/terms and prefers continuing revision support over a one-time deliverable. | The professor only wants a one-time workbook/course or would use free institutional support instead. |
| The trust controls are differentiating. | Approved-tool checks, source bounds, named review, fallback, and explicit approval affect the purchase decision. | The controls feel like generic copy, unnecessary friction, or are weaker than institutional requirements. |

## Safety boundary

Use only the professor's own assignment instructions, learning objectives, rubric structure, public or approved institutional guidance, and non-sensitive notes. Do not enter student submissions, names, grades, accommodations, protected research, or other identifiable/restricted information. Do not recommend AI detection or automate grading, misconduct findings, learning-objective decisions, or final approval.

If the professor's institution prohibits the selected tool or use, stop the pilot and record the policy mismatch. A manual, institution-approved fallback must remain available.

## Materials

- A laptop with the local Workflow Lab prototype.
- The assignment instructions, learning objectives, rubric, and relevant institutional/department AI guidance.
- A timer.
- This protocol and the evidence table below.
- A new copy of the exported intake and package for each run. Do not overwrite the first-run evidence.

## Moderator opening script

> We are testing the workflow, not you. Please work as you normally would and say what you expect before each action. I will not teach you how to use the product unless you are completely blocked. We will not use student work or sensitive information. The product is a local prototype; its time target is an estimate, not a promise.

## Session 1 — baseline, intake, package, and real complaint

### 1. Baseline interview — 10 minutes

Ask without suggesting answers:

1. What triggers you to revisit this assignment?
2. Walk me through what you did the last time, including tools, handoffs, checks, and rework.
3. Which learning objective must not be weakened?
4. What current AI policy or student behavior creates the most uncertainty or work?
5. Approximately how many minutes did the last redesign/review take? What is that estimate based on?
6. What failure would make a redesign unusable?
7. What do you currently pay for personally, if anything, to solve this job? Why that and not another option?

Record the current steps before showing the product. Do not convert a guess into a precise baseline.

### 2. Uncoached intake — target observation, not a pass/fail time

Give the professor the prototype at `/intake` and say only:

> Describe the recurring process you use to revise this assignment when policy, student behavior, or teaching needs change. Use the real assignment, but omit student and restricted data. Stop when you believe the package is ready.

Observe silently. Record:

- start and completion time;
- fields that cause hesitation or require explanation;
- policy/privacy warnings noticed or missed;
- any point where the professor abandons accurate detail to finish faster;
- whether package generation succeeds without intervention.

### 3. First package evaluation — 15 minutes

Ask the professor to inspect the operating steps, assets, review points, failure conditions, and measurement plan, then answer:

1. What would you actually use next?
2. What is generic, wrong, unsafe, or missing?
3. Does this preserve the learning objective and your authority?
4. Which approved tools and policies does it fit or violate?
5. Rate usefulness from 1 to 5. What prevents the next higher rating?
6. Could you run this without help? Why or why not?

### 4. Genuine complaint and approval decision

The professor must report the most consequential real miss, not a moderator-created example. Capture the failed step, category, what happened, desired outcome, usefulness, actual time when known, and corrections made.

Let Workflow Lab draft one correction. Ask the professor to review current and proposed instructions side by side. The professor must choose one:

- approve and replace the active package;
- keep the current package;
- neither, because the draft is unsafe or does not resolve the need.

Record the reason verbatim. Do not treat approval as success unless the professor can explain why the correction meets the need without weakening safeguards.

## Between sessions

The professor uses the approved package on one real, low-risk assignment-redesign task. They record actual minutes by step, corrections, help needed, policy conflicts, and the output they produced. The moderator does not repair the workflow during the run.

Schedule Session 2 within 7–14 days. Keep the first package, complaint, proposed correction, approval decision, and second-run package as separate evidence.

## Session 2 — repeatability, outcome, and willingness to pay

### 1. Independent second run

Ask the professor to repeat the workflow without setup help. Record:

- completion and actual minutes by step;
- corrections and help points;
- whether the accepted revision resolved the original complaint;
- any new critical failure;
- usefulness from 1 to 5;
- whether they would choose this process again for the next assignment.

Do not claim a time improvement unless the baseline and both runs are comparable and the professor confirms the comparison is fair.

### 2. Willingness-to-pay interview

Keep these questions separate from usability feedback. Ask in order and do not reveal competitor pricing first.

1. If this disappeared tomorrow, what would you do instead?
2. What part, if any, is valuable enough to pay for personally?
3. Would you prefer a one-time finished workflow package, an ongoing subscription that improves accepted workflows after each run, institution-provided access, or none? Why?
4. What result would have to be demonstrated before you would pay?
5. What would make you cancel after one month?
6. At what monthly price would this feel too cheap to trust, a reasonable personal purchase, expensive but possible, and too expensive?
7. Would you take a concrete next step now: start a paid pilot when available, introduce the departmental buyer, join another unpaid test, or stop?

Only after the unaided answers, show the market anchors: focused one-time resources at roughly $29–$49, low-cost courses from about $19, and a professor-specific workflow competitor at $59/month. Ask whether those anchors change the preferred format or value threshold. This is research, not an offer or pricing change.

## Precommitted decision rules

One pilot can expose defects and weaken assumptions; it cannot validate a market. Use the following rules after five comparable professor pilots:

### Continue the subscription thesis

- at least 4 of 5 complete intake and package generation without moderator intervention;
- at least 4 of 5 rate the tested package at least 4/5 after the second run;
- no unresolved critical privacy or academic-control defect;
- at least 3 of 5 submit a genuine complaint and judge an approved correction useful on the next run;
- at least 3 of 5 prefer ongoing revision support over a one-time artifact or free substitute;
- at least 2 of 5 make a concrete payment-oriented commitment, not just say the idea sounds useful.

### Change or narrow

- the workflow is useful only in one discipline, course type, or policy condition;
- professors value the artifact but not continued revision;
- the institutional buyer or teaching center is consistently the payer;
- the main value is policy translation, assignment design, or review workload rather than the broader workflow diagnosis.

### Pause or kill this wedge

- fewer than 3 of 5 can name a current painful assignment-redesign job;
- fewer than 3 of 5 can complete a second run without operator help;
- usefulness remains below 4/5 after corrections;
- free institutional support already solves the job adequately;
- no participant makes a payment-oriented commitment;
- the workflow requires sensitive data, AI detection, or academic decisions that cannot be handled safely.

## Immediate product-change rule

Fix a verified critical safety or completion defect before the next pilot. Otherwise, do not build a new assignment-specific template, pricing page, account system, persistence layer, external AI integration, or billing behavior until at least one real professor has completed both sessions and the evidence shows exactly what failed.
